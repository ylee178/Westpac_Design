Q2 · BUSINESS vs PERSONAL

Business lending is a different animal

Personal lending is a bet on an individual's salary and credit score. Business lending is a bet on a business entity's cash generation — sole trader · company · partnership · trust · corporate.

Why harder: ① legally complex structures (company-owned-by-family-trust) ② amounts are bigger and custom ($500K to tens of millions) ③ analysis reads financials, industry conditions, collateral, guarantees, even board composition — not a single credit score.

This is where banks compete hardest on relationships. A dedicated banker per customer is the norm — and BizEdge exists to support that depth at scale.

Q3 · THE SECOND SIDE

Critical finding: BizEdge is a two-sided platform

BizEdge is not purely a banker tool. It has a customer-facing surface where business customers pre-fill forms and track application status in real time. Rich Data Co describes its contribution verbatim:

"a two-sided digital finance application form, which allows both customers and bankers to jointly work on an application."

This finding grounds Decision 7 (three-way task ownership) — the "customer" actor is not an abstract design construct but a real surface inside the actual BizEdge architecture.

Q4 · WHAT IS A LOS

The "operating system" a banker uses to create a loan

Before modern LOS: bankers assembled loans across email, Word, spreadsheets, and legacy systems. McKinsey calls this "swivel chair work" — literally swiveling your chair to the next monitor to retype data. It's exactly how errors happen.

A modern LOS consolidates customer data, credit, approval, documents, and booking into one coordinated workspace. Major vendors: nCino (market leader), Moody's Lending Suite, Baker Hill, Hawthorn River. Westpac built BizEdge in-house but partners with Rich Data Co for AI.

CHAPTER 2 · REGULATORY BACKDROP

AUSTRAC, AML/CTF, 2026 reform, and Westpac's scar

Westpac's regulatory history isn't neutral. It paid Australia's largest corporate fine, rebuilt its compliance backbone over five years, and the 2026 AUSTRAC reform commenced two weeks before this interview — all of which gives specific weight to my design.

Q8 · AUSTRAC

Australia's AML/CTF regulator — sets the rules, banks comply

Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre. Every Australian bank is a "reporting entity" required to identify customers, monitor transactions, report suspicious matters, keep records, and maintain an AML/CTF program. Banks don't negotiate.

Analogy: AUSTRAC isn't the police arresting troublemakers — it's the regulator telling the nightclub how to check IDs, train bouncers, and flag suspicious activity. It supervises the processes banks use to keep criminals out of the financial system.

The issuer of Australia's largest-ever corporate civil penalty: A$1.3B against Westpac in 2020.

Q9 · AML/CTF BASICS

"Forcing the bank to notice"

Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Terrorism Financing. Money laundering = obscuring the origin of illegal funds. Terrorism financing = moving (often small) amounts to fund violence. Both depend on the financial system not noticing.

In regulated banking, compliance and UX are not separate concerns — they're the same concern at different layers. Every deal-setup decision affects how evidence is captured, verified, and made available for audit.

Q10 · CDD

Customer Due Diligence — radically more complex for business

"Do I really know who this customer is? Do I understand their ML risk?" Individual CDD is simple. Business CDD requires:

① Identify the entity (company/sole trader/trust/partnership) ② Identify the people acting (directors/signatories) ③ Identify beneficial owners (look-through to real humans) ④ Understand the nature/purpose of the relationship ⑤ Assess ML risk ⑥ Document evidence AND reasoning.

Under the 2026 reform, CDD splits into initial CDD (onboarding) and ongoing CDD (continuous monitoring) — the split that creates the transition trap (Q14).

Q11 · BENEFICIAL OWNER

Look through the corporate structure to real humans

Example: Sunrise Holdings Pty Ltd → Coastal Investments Ltd → Smith Family Trust → trustee → Mr Smith, wife, daughter. Stop at the first layer and you never know who's really in charge. AUSTRAC requires look-through for ownership or control above 25%.

For a senior banker who mostly handles sole traders, a trust with multiple beneficiaries is the exact rare case where muscle memory fails. Missing a beneficial owner is one of the most common senior-banker CDD errors — the scenario Pain 2 targets.

Q12 · ENTITY TYPE

AUSTRAC's five categories ground Decision 1

AUSTRAC's initial CDD guidance groups customers into: Individual · Sole trader · Body corporate / Partnership / Unincorporated association · Trust · Government body. Each type fundamentally changes what the banker must verify.

The regulator itself models the problem as "entity type drives required steps." A static checklist showing every possible step is overwhelming; one showing only common-case steps silently skips rare cases. Only a dynamic checklist that reshapes by product × entity × jurisdiction matches the regulator's own model.

Q13 · 2026 REFORM

"From compliance-based to risk-based, outcomes-oriented"

Commenced 31 March 2026 (two weeks before this interview). Old prescriptive tick-the-box regime → banks now assess each customer's risk and decide evidence sufficiency on "reasonable grounds." AUSTRAC's own words, verbatim:

"from a compliance-based approach to a risk-based, outcomes-oriented approach."

Analogy: the old driving test scored 20 specific mechanical actions. The new test says "drive safely from A to B and document your judgments." Harder, not easier.

Senior banker muscle memory was partly invalidated two weeks ago — the direct source of Pain 2's urgency.

Q14 · TRANSITION TRAP

A single deal can span two frameworks

Initial CDD: existing entities can keep using the pre-reform procedure ("legacy ACIP") until 31 March 2029. Three-year runway.

Ongoing CDD: no transition period. New risk-based framework applies immediately from 31 March 2026.

A senior banker thinking "we have until 2029" is partly right and partly wrong — their initial CDD is fine, their ongoing monitoring is not. This is exactly where muscle memory becomes a liability. Decision 8 (legacy/reform mode indicator) addresses this directly.

Q15 · 2020 WESTPAC CASE

Australia's largest corporate fine — A$1.3 Billion

November 2019 AUSTRAC proceedings, 23M+ admitted contraventions. The core failures:

① 19.5M+ IFTIs ($11B+ value) unreported over ~5 years ② Source-of-funds not passed to downstream banks ③ 76,000+ additional contraventions ④ Failure to monitor for child-exploitation-linked transactions (initially 12 customers, then 250 more identified).

21 October 2020: Federal Court orders A$1.3B. Westpac spent five years rebuilding AML/CTF controls, data, policies, and processes.

For my design: Decision 6 (provenance) and Decision 8 (mode indicator) are not generic UX choices — they respond to a regulatory history Westpac knows intimately and has publicly committed to learning from.

CHAPTER 3 · THE THREE PAINS IN THE BRIEF

What these pains actually look like in real work

The actual behaviour behind the brief's one-line framings — the patterns, the examples, and the cognitive mechanism that explains each one.

Q16 · PAIN 1 — NEW BANKER ERRORS

Not dramatic failures — slow accumulation of small process errors

① Entity misclassification: a new banker classifies a "family business" as a sole trader when it's actually a discretionary trust with a corporate trustee. This single misclassification invalidates all downstream CDD.

② Incomplete data capture: one required field missed — a beneficial owner, a source-of-funds declaration, a specific document. Deal proceeds → credit review → kicked back → resubmit days later.

③ Skipped verification: banker captures the driver's license but forgets second-form verification for a higher-risk customer. Compliance catches it, customer forms a poor first impression.

④ Misjudged risk: "standard risk" when it should be enhanced due diligence — missing foreign exposure or a PEP in the ownership chain.

Sources: McKinsey commercial banking onboarding research · Cognitive Group RBC study (88 error types, ~$30M/year)

Q17 · PAIN 2 — SENIOR BANKER SKIP

Expertise reversal effect — where experience becomes a weakness

Cognitive psychology: expert performance relies on schemas (encoded patterns). For common products the schemas are finely tuned; for rare products they're out of date or don't fit.

Concrete example: a senior who has done thousands of term loans opens a bank guarantee deal, thinks "same process," and muscle memory kicks in. But guarantees require different things — beneficiary legal name and address, expiry aligned with underlying contract, term deposit as security, paper issuance from a physical branch. Documentation review bounces it back.

Other examples: applying "company" mental model to a trust structure; applying company-style ownership logic to a partnership.

Australian context: 3 of the 4 major Australian banks have stopped offering bank guarantees to retail customers. Westpac's remaining guarantee business is a specialised offering, making rare-product skip especially acute.

Academic grounding: expertise reversal effect (well-studied in aviation and medicine)

Q18 · PAIN 3 — NO SINGLE VIEW

Not a dashboard — a single guided workflow surface

Priya scenario: partway through a business loan, she needs to check three things right now — PPSR returned? Financials uploaded? Risk rating approved? Without a single view, each answer lives in a different system — 10–30 seconds of cognitive load per switch. McKinsey calls this "swivel chair work." Twenty times per deal × five deals per day = a huge attention tax.

The reframe that changed my design: "no single view" does NOT mean "she needs a unified dashboard." Modern LOS research (nCino · Moody's · Baker Hill · Hawthorn River) converges: "single view = single guided workflow surface" — information sequenced around the next action, not displayed in parallel.

→ Decision 5: progress spine with stage-specific checklist. Not a parallel dashboard.

CHAPTER 4 · DESIGN DECISIONS

Eight decisions — What · Problem · Rationale · One-breath

Each decision targets one or more of the three pains, and each one has its own chain of evidence.

D1 · TARGETS PAIN 1+2

Dynamic checklist — reshapes by product × entity × jurisdiction

What: when the banker selects product type, entity type, and jurisdictional factors, the checklist automatically reshapes to show the specific mandatory steps for that combination.

Why: AUSTRAC itself structures its guidance by entity type — the regulator already models it this way. Hawthorn River: "validation rules will vary based on the structure of the loan, borrowers, and collateral." Not a Westpac-specific bet — an industry principle.

One-breath: "Every business loan is a different combination, so the checklist rearranges itself to show you only the steps that matter for the deal you're working on."

D2 · AUSTRAC-ALIGNED

Skip as friction, not a hard block, with logged reason

What: when a banker tries to pass an incomplete item, a lightweight dialog asks for a one-line reason and logs it for audit. No hard block.

Why: hard blocks create workarounds; silent skips create audit gaps. Friction-with-reason is the middle path. AUSTRAC's post-reform guidance: "Some initial CDD steps can be delayed where essential to avoid interrupting ordinary business." My design operationalises the regulator's own intent.

One-breath: "Instead of blocking the banker, the system asks for a one-line reason and records it — respecting judgment while creating an audit trail compliance and the regulator can both read."

D3 · TARGETS PAIN 1

Inline expandable knowledge, not a separate chatbot

What: each checklist item has an "i" affordance that expands inline within the row — why this step, common mistakes for this specific combination, policy links. Default collapsed.

Why: a separate chatbot fails new bankers — they must leave the workflow, re-explain context, and formulate a question they don't yet know how to ask. Psychological safety research (Edmondson, via Chartered Banker Institute): juniors delay asking due to reputation risk. Inline expansion removes the social cost.

One-breath: "Each checklist item has a small expandable help button built in, so a banker can see why something's required without leaving the workflow."

D4 · NN/G-GROUNDED

Progressive disclosure (no Learning/Expert mode toggle)

What: no mode toggle. Every banker sees the same interface. Experience-level adaptation happens via contextual expansion, not self-declaration.

Why: mode toggles produce four bad outcomes — stigma, false confidence, decision paralysis, discoverability problems. And a deeper insight: expertise is product-specific, not banker-general. A senior may be expert on term loans and novice on trust lending. A binary toggle can't capture that granularity; contextual expansion can.

One-breath: "Instead of asking the banker to declare 'I'm a beginner,' everyone just expands help on any step they need — seniors stay compact on common products and expand only on the rare ones."

D5 · BIGGEST REFRAME

Progress spine as guided workflow surface, not a dashboard

What: a horizontal progress spine at the top (Setup → Identification → Credit → Approval → Settlement) with the current stage emphasised, and below it the dynamic checklist for the current stage only.

Why: I initially assumed "single view = unified dashboard." Four major LOS vendors (nCino · Moody's · Baker Hill · Hawthorn River) all converge on "single view = single guided workflow surface." Information sequenced around the next action, not displayed in parallel. This was the biggest single direction change in my design.

One-breath: "Instead of a dashboard with everything, a horizontal progress bar with the current stage highlighted, and below it only what you need to do right now."

D6 · "REASONABLE GROUNDS"

Provenance indicators for auto-filled data

What: every field auto-populated from external sources (Company search · PPSR · customer portal) shows a small indicator — source + timestamp + confidence. Hover reveals details; banker can override, and overrides are logged alongside the original.

Why: the reform requires decisions to be established on "reasonable grounds." "The system told me" is not reasonable grounds. The 2020 case is a reminder of what happens when evidence visibility is weak at scale — five years later you're paying A$1.3B.

One-breath: "When the system auto-fills a field, a tiny indicator shows where it came from and when — so the banker can see whether to trust it or override, and auditors can see exactly how each decision was made."

D7 · BIZEDGE ARCHITECTURE

Three-way task ownership — Banker / System / Customer

What: every outstanding item shows an owner indicator. The banker can filter to "my actions only."

Why: "outstanding" isn't one bucket — some items wait on the banker, some on the system, some on the customer. Collapsing these makes the banker chase items they can't personally act on. The decisive evidence: RDC's "two-sided digital finance application form" — the customer is not an abstract actor but a real BizEdge surface. Three-way ownership mirrors the actual shape of the platform. This was flagged as my weakest decision in audit feedback; the RDC finding turned it around.

One-breath: "Every task shows who owns it, so the banker can filter to their own actions instead of chasing items they're waiting on other people or the system for."

D8 · TRANSITION TRAP

Legacy ACIP vs reform CDD mode indicator

What: a persistent indicator top-right — "Initial CDD: Legacy ACIP (transition)" or "Initial CDD: Reform framework." Ongoing CDD is always under the reform and noted separately. Clickable: reveals why this framework applies and when the deal must transition.

Why: the transition trap (Q14) means a single deal can span two frameworks. Seniors may assume one framework applies uniformly because "we have until 2029." Ongoing CDD changed two weeks ago. Making mode explicit prevents silent misapplication. A temporary artifact (only until March 2029), but a critical safeguard during the three-year transition.

One-breath: "A small persistent label shows which AUSTRAC framework applies to this deal's initial CDD — so seniors don't silently apply their old mental models during the three-year window when some deals span both frameworks."

CHAPTER 5 · STRATEGIC STORY

Why now, why this role, and the interview-day framing

Beyond the pains and decisions — the three forces that make this role the highest-leverage design job in Australian business banking right now.

Q19 · BANKER HIRING

350 new business bankers in 2 years → learning-curve bottleneck

Timeline: November 2024 "200 by end-2027" commitment → September 2025 target expanded to "350 over two years," with 135 already hired (Reuters, citing Paul Fowler briefing).

Business case for my design: McKinsey — new commercial bankers take 30–100 days to reach productivity, primarily due to tooling fragmentation. 350 × learning curve = enormous bottleneck. Every day a new banker produces compliant deals faster is real revenue for Westpac. V1 checklist targets this curve directly — not by improving training, but by making the tool do more of the "when and why" work training can't solve.

Q20 · UNITE FRAMING

"My design rides the UNITE wave, not fights it"

① Not layered over legacy mess: BizEdge sits over a platform that's actively getting simpler. Each of the 180+ retired applications removed friction my design would otherwise need to compensate for.

② Devil's-advocate defense: "UX can't fix a bank's culture" → "I'm not claiming UX fixes culture. I'm claiming my design rides the UNITE wave. 180+ · 70%+ · 700+ — UNITE is delivering, and my design operationalises that progress at the banker's workstation." Specific + humble.

③ Strategic alignment signal: UNITE is back-end simplification; my design is the front-end translation of that simplification into daily banker experience.

Q21 · MARTIN ANDERSON — EXECUTIVE BACKING

Convergent evidence for the V2 AI teammate thesis

Dr Martin Anderson (Head of Technology, Business Lending & B&W UNITE; 2025 Team of the Year), AWS Financial Services Symposium Sydney:

"People are going to be surprised by how these sequences are going to be reorganised, resequenced, rewired. It's not just going to be a case of adding in AI-specific points; it's actually reimagining the process so that you can actually optimise it to leverage AI … the whole end-to-end sequence of business lending can be optimised."

I arrived at V2 (AI ambient teammate) independently — from cognitive psychology and my own experience building a Slack-based AI companion. Then I found Anderson's quote. Not borrowed authority — convergent evidence. Protects V2 from the "too speculative for a take-home" pushback.
